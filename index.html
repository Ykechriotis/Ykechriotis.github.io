Line Follower

I built a line following robot. I used SOLIDWORKS to design custom laser cut and 3D printed parts, and KiCAD to design PCB's for my microcontrollers and other components. The robot uses a Raspberry Pi Pico W to read a downward facing camera and display its view on an OLED screen. The camera data is sent to a PIC32 microcontroller, which sends PWM signals to the drive motors based on a PID feedback loop.

Swarm Robot Behaviors

I simulated three robot swarm behaviors. The first uses "hop count" to determine a coordinate system and display a Northwestern N. The second sorts the robots into cocentric rings based on the Brazil nut effect. The third simulates Reynold's flocking. I also implemented the flocking behavior on a small swarm of real robots.

These robots exhibit a Reynold's flocking behavior. They adjust their trajectories based on the sum of separation, cohesion, and alignment vectors, resulting in a coordinated flock.

The robots in this simulated swarm do not know their absolute position from the start, except for two "node" robots on the bottom corners. The robots form a coordinate system by sending "hop count" messages to their neighbors that tell them how far away form each node they are. Using these hop counts, each robot can triangulate their approximate coordinates, and use them to decide whether or not they should light up to form the Northwestern N or not.



These robots sort themselves based on their virtual sizes using the Brazil nut effect. The Brazil nut effect states that when a system of irregularly sized objects is subjected to some sort of shaking or vibration, the smallest objects will end up closest to whatever point of attraction exists. These robots model the effect by constantly updating their velocity vectors based on the sum of a gravity, repulsion, and random vector. After some time, the robots sort into cocentric rings based on size.



KUKA youBot Pick-and-Place Simulation

A simulation of the KUKA youBot performing a pick-and-place motion. All code was written in MATLAB using the Modern Robotics code library. The code generates the end effector trajectory based on the desired pick-and-place locations, then uses a PI feedback controller to apply joint forces resulting in correct end-effector twists to follow the trajectory.

Duckietown Intersection Navigation

For my final project for the class Self Driving Cars with Duckietown at ETH Zurich, I programmed an autonomous intersection navigation behavior that allows multiple cars to safely navigate intersections.

The duckiebots can follow lane markings using a histogram filter for state estimation. Using their camera, they can detect the red intersection markings and determine the available turn options. I trained a YOLOv5 object detection model on images of duckiebots to be able to recognize when another bot is at an intersection. Whenever a duckiebot is detected on the right side of the screen, the bot will wait at the intersection until the other bot moves. When the intersection is clear, the duckiebot will execute its action, using PID control based on the wheel motor encoder readings to achieve a desired turn or straight action.

The duckiebots detect intersections by applying a gaussian filter and a red color mask. Then, based on the number and location of red (white) intersection markers detected, they determine the available actions for the intersection (straight, right, left).

Multi-Agent Planner

For one of my other classes at ETH Zurich, Planning and Decision making for Autonomous Robots, I programmed a multi agent path planner for differential drive robots. While I initially tried using an RRT based planner, what ended up working best was a grid based approach. The robots construct an intial path to their respective goals, and then recalculate their plan whenever they encounter another agent on their path.

Haptic Telemanipulation

For my capstone project at Northwestern University, my team built a haptic finger and thumb interface to teleoperate a robot finger and thumb and perform dexterous tasks. The goal was to achieve high stiffness bilateral control between the two systems. I was mainly responsible for the mechanical design of the haptic finger.

The Haptic Finger

Our design had three rotational degrees of freedom. Two were actuated (MP and IP joint), and one passive at the fingertip.

CAD Model

We used cable drive transmissions to achieve zero backlash, low friction actuation. The main disadvantage of cable drives, their limited range of motion, was not an issue since we don't want the system to overextend the user's finger in any way, so the joint limits were a benefit. The design features nested cable wheels that rotate independently on a fixed shaft. This saved space and kept forces restricted mostly to one plane, although the assembly process was made a little more difficult.

Cable drives

For motors, we chose Maxon ECX Torque 22XL motors for their high torque, high torque constant, and very low rotor inertia. We wanted to keep inertia in our system as low as possible to maximize torque transparency for the end user. These brushless motors are controlled using field oriented control with magnetic encoders.

With our combined haptic/robot finger/thumb interface, we achieved a 500 N/m virtual object stiffness (enough to tell the difference in hardness between different objects during teleoperation), and could complete various dextrous tasks such as a pinch grip, and unscrewing a vial cap.
